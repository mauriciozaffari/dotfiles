# Naming Types Reference

A great naming session explores multiple categories. Here are the primary naming archetypes, with examples and guidance on when each works best.

---

## 1. Evocative / Emotional
Names that evoke a feeling, atmosphere, or emotion — not the literal product.

**Examples:** Notion, Stripe, Drift, Asana, Loom, Slack, Figma

**Best for:** Brands that want to own a feeling. Works across virtually every category.

**Tips:**
- Think about the core emotion (calm, power, delight, speed, trust)
- Draw from nature, music, texture, movement
- Test: does hearing the name trigger any emotional response?

---

## 2. Invented / Coined Words
Made-up words, portmanteaus, or transformations that are entirely ownable.

**Examples:** Kodak, Xerox, Spotify, Skype, Rolex, Verizon, Hulu

**Best for:** Brands that need global reach, strong trademark, no baggage

**Construction methods:**
- **Portmanteau:** Combine two meaningful words (e.g., Pinterest = Pin + Interest)
- **Morpheme play:** Use meaningful roots (bio-, neo-, lux-, etc.)
- **Letter swap:** Replace vowels or add/remove letters for distinctiveness
- **Sound design:** Engineer a name that sounds like what it feels like

---

## 3. Metaphor / Analogy
Names borrowed from a completely different domain that creates a resonant comparison.

**Examples:** Amazon (vast, powerful), Apple (human, simple), Stripe (a fundamental pattern), Firefly (small but lights the way), Ribbon (connects things beautifully)

**Best for:** Companies with a big or abstract vision. Creates instant narrative.

**Tips:**
- The best metaphors have layered meanings — don't over-explain them
- Animal names tend to be powerful and cross-culturally resonant (Mammoth, Jaguar, Robin)
- Nature metaphors convey scale, growth, or timelessness

---

## 4. Founder / Eponymous
Named after a person — founder, historical figure, or archetype.

**Examples:** Ford, Disney, Gucci, Salesforce (not a person but feels like authority), Tesla

**Best for:** Founder-led brands where personal reputation is the product. Premium/luxury segments.

**Caution:** Ties the brand to a person permanently. Hard to transfer. Use intentionally.

---

## 5. Descriptive / Functional
Names that tell you what the product does, literally.

**Examples:** Basecamp, Zoom, Dropbox, Snapchat, Salesforce

**Best for:** Early-stage products where clarity beats cleverness. SEO-friendly.

**Caution:** Harder to trademark. Easier to imitate. May constrain the brand long-term.

**Tip:** If you go descriptive, pair it with a strong visual identity and brand personality to add the emotional layer the name lacks.

---

## 6. Experiential / Verb-Friendly
Names designed to become verbs or behaviors in natural language.

**Examples:** Google (googling), Uber (Uber me), Zoom (let's Zoom), Tweet (tweeting)

**Best for:** Consumer products with high frequency of use. Network effect businesses.

**The test:** Does using the product naturally generate a new verb? If yes, the name has language network effect potential.

---

## 7. Abstract / Meaningless (Designed)
Names with no inherent meaning — pure sound design.

**Examples:** Kodak, Häagen-Dazs (invented Scandinavian-sounding words), Accenture, Agilent

**Best for:** Large enterprises where meaning must be fully constructed through marketing.

**Caution:** Expensive to build meaning into a blank-slate name. Requires heavy brand investment.

---

## 8. Place / Geographic Reference
Names that borrow from a location — real or imagined.

**Examples:** Amazon, Patagonia, Klarna (Swedish), Atlassian

**Best for:** Brands that want to inherit the associations of a place (vast, rugged, global).

**Tip:** The best place names work because of the feeling of the place, not the literal geography.

---

## 9. Number / Symbol
Uses numbers, punctuation, or non-letter characters as core identity elements.

**Examples:** 3M, 7-Eleven, Web.com, Y Combinator (YC)

**Best for:** Established brands rebranding, or technical/industrial segments.

**Caution:** Can be hard to say out loud and SEO-unfriendly. Use sparingly.

---

## 10. Portmanteau / Mashup
Combines two existing words to create a new concept-word.

**Examples:** Pinterest (Pin + Interest), Instagram (Instant + Telegram), YouTube (You + Tube), Microsoft (Micro + Soft)

**Best for:** When two concepts are truly core to the brand and neither alone is sufficient.

**Tips:**
- The seam should feel natural, not forced
- Test pronunciation — mashups can get weird in the mouth
- Aim for one clear stress pattern

---

## Naming Category Decision Matrix

| If the brand needs to feel... | Consider categories... |
|---|---|
| Bold, powerful, scaled | Metaphor, Animal, Invented |
| Warm, human, approachable | Evocative, Experiential |
| Trustworthy, established | Eponymous, Geographic |
| Cutting-edge, technical | Invented, Abstract |
| Simple, clear, accessible | Descriptive, Portmanteau |
| Viral, behavior-changing | Experiential/Verb-Friendly |
